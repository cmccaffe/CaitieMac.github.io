Research

My research focuses on structural characterization of endogenous protein complexes from native cell/organelle extracts. To do so, I work on methods to assemble protein complexes from multi-resolution experimental data and predictions. Currently, I am working on applying these methods to the ciliary proteome of tetrahymena.

Publications

Below you will find selected publications. An extensive list of my publications can be found on my CV.

The protein organization of a red blood cell.

We define the canonical RBC proteome and interactome using mass spectrometry. We validated protein complexes using chemical crosslinking and cryo-electron microscopy. using our chemical crosslinking data, we reconstruct the ankyrin complex and reveal its dynamic state may explain the changes in RBC morphology.

Improving integrtive modeling into low- to medium-resolution electron microscopy structures with evolutioanry couplings.

We incorporate the co‐evolution of intermolecular amino acids as a new type of distance restraint in the integrative modeling platform in order to build three‐dimensional models of atomic structures into EM maps.

Simplified geometric representations of protein structures identify complementary interfaces.

We show that a reduced representation of protein surfaces can accurately predict protein-protein interaction interfaces from optimizing the complementarity between physicochemical properties on surface patches between the proteins.

Structural biology in the multi-omics era.

We share our perspective on the recent advancements in cryo-electron microscopy and the path leading towards using this tool for high-throughput structural biology.

About

I grew up in Stamford, CT, right outside of New York City. For most of my childhood I had dreams of becoming an actress and and spent one summmer taking the train into the city every day to attend acting school. In high school, I realized that I happened to be particaularly good at both math and chemistry despite my lack of study skills. I figured, statistically speaking, that the odds of me becoming a successful scientist were much higher than those of me becoming a successful actress and my love for science and math were born. I went on to received my Bachelors of Science in Chemistry from the University of Maryland in 2015 where I minored in mathematics and Spanish. My undergraduate research focused dispersant development for oil spill remediation and shape-changing hydrogels.

After graduation, I moved to Washington D.C, where I spent two years as postbaccalaureate fellow at the National Institutes of Health. I worked at the National Eye Institute in the Protein Biochemistry and Molecular Modeling group with Dr. Yuri Sergeev. At the NIH I developed the unfolding mutational screen, which predicted misfolding mutations based on protein stability and correlated these mutations with inherited eye disease. On the weekends, I worked for a STEM outreach startup developing and teaching exciting science courses for elementary school aged students. As part of the start up I was very involved in developing and leading courses targeted at engaging girls in STEM related fields.

In the fall of 2017, I joined the University of Texas at Austin graduate program. Find more about what I do here in the research section! When not in the lab (computational or ) I enjoy an active lifestyle with my partner (Paul) and dog (Sybil). Whether it be running, biking, or hiking, I am happiest when I am outdoors. I also find peace in indoor bouldering and painting (check out my artwork section!). I find my artistic inspiration from my research and the intersection of the natural and mechanical world.
